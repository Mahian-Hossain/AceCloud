Stop A/B testing button colours. Test pricing pages.

The single highest-leverage page on the marketing site is also the one most teams treat as untouchable. The pricing page is where the company decides who it sells to. Test it like it matters.

The economics of experimentation are perverse. Most teams run their twenty experiments a year on the homepage hero, the nav layout, the colour of the trial button. Each test moves a leading metric by half a percent. The pricing page, where the actual conversion happens, ships in launch week and is never touched again.

The lift on a serious pricing page rewrite is regularly 20% to 80% in pipeline. The lift on a button-colour test is 1% to 3% in clicks. We know which one is worth running. We almost never do.

Why teams won't touch the pricing page.

Three reasons, in our experience. Pricing is owned by sales, finance and the CEO simultaneously, which means it's owned by nobody and any change requires three approvals. Pricing changes leak into customer conversations and feel high-stakes. And the marketing team is rarely permitted to A/B test pricing because finance worries about reporting integrity. So the page sits.

What we test there instead.

You don't have to test the prices. You can test almost everything around the prices: the framing of tiers, the order of features, the anchor pricing, the comparison table, the "talk to sales" threshold, the trial-vs-demo path, the social proof above the table, the FAQ below it. Almost every one of these moves the conversion rate measurably and none of them require finance signoff.

The pricing page is where you stop being a brand and start being a price. Treat that moment with the seriousness it deserves.

A worked example.

One B2B client had a three-tier pricing page with the middle tier highlighted as "most popular". We replaced the highlight with a per-seat ROI calculator that showed payback period in months for the visitor's company size. Identical prices. Identical features. The change moved demo-request rate from 2.1% to 5.4% over a six-week test, p < 0.01. That is the kind of lift a year of homepage tests cannot produce.

How to run it.

Treat the pricing page as a product. Two experiments a quarter, minimum. Server-side variants so the SEO doesn't bend. A pre-registered hypothesis. A leading metric and a guardrail metric. A two-week minimum window. A written post-mortem regardless of result. Most teams won't do this because it's tedious. Which is exactly why doing it is leverage.
