SEO is dead. Long live discoverability — across Google, Perplexity, ChatGPT and the open web.

Half our 2025 traffic for one client came from being cited inside ChatGPT and Perplexity responses. The other half came from Google. The discipline isn't "SEO" anymore — it's discoverability across a fragmented set of answer engines.

The premise we were sold for fifteen years — "rank for the keyword, get the click" — is fraying in real time. Google's AI Overviews now answer roughly 40% of informational queries above the fold. Perplexity, ChatGPT browse mode, Claude search and Bing Copilot collectively serve millions of "research" queries a day that never produce a single classical organic click.

That doesn't mean organic visibility is dead. It means the surface has multiplied, and the rules per surface are different.

The four surfaces that now matter.

For every client engagement, we now plan visibility across four distinct surfaces, each with its own optimisation playbook:

  • Google classical organic. Still the largest single source of intent-led traffic. Still rewards technical hygiene, page experience, and topical authority. Just less than it used to.
  • Google AI Overviews. A summarisation layer that cites a small set of sources. Optimised by being concise, structured, and the canonical answer to a question — not by stuffing the keyword.
  • LLM browse mode (Perplexity, ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot). A different ranking surface entirely. Citations depend on cleanliness of structure, recency, and whether the model can extract a quotable claim.
  • The open web of newsletters, podcasts and Reddit. The most under-priced surface of 2026. LLMs cite Reddit and Substack disproportionately. So do humans.

The new technical hygiene.

Classical technical SEO still matters — Core Web Vitals, schema, canonicalisation, internal linking. But the LLM-era checklist adds new items. Pages need to be extractable: clear H2 questions, short paragraphs, definitions in the first sentence, structured data that confirms the page's claim. The new "first paragraph" rule isn't "front-load the keyword" — it's "answer the question in 30 words so a model can quote you".

You're no longer writing for a human reader. You're writing for a model that decides whether to introduce you to the human reader.

Programmatic content, done with taste.

Programmatic landing pages get a bad reputation because most implementations are SEO sludge. Done with discipline — clean schema, real underlying data, a brand voice, a useful page for the human who actually arrives — they remain the single highest-leverage organic growth lever for B2B and marketplace clients.

For one fintech client, a 4,200-page programmatic system covering "[city] incorporation guide" pages drives 38% of inbound demo requests in 2026. It also gets cited by Perplexity in roughly 14% of related queries. The same content asset, two surfaces, compounding.

What we stopped doing.

We stopped chasing head-term rankings as the goal metric. We stopped writing the kind of 2,400-word "ultimate guide" SEO content that LLMs were trained on, then plagiarised, and now strip-mine for facts. We stopped reporting "keyword positions" without their citation share inside the AI answer.

We started reporting LLM citation share alongside organic rankings. We started writing tighter, more quotable, more definitive content — the kind a model wants to cite, and a human wants to read. We started treating Reddit and newsletters as a paid-equivalent surface.

The honest take.

If your SEO consultant is still selling you a quarterly report on the rankings of forty keywords, they're working in a discipline that no longer exists. Discoverability is now multi-surface, citation-aware and editorially demanding. It's also more interesting than SEO has been in a decade. There's a lot of leverage left.
